FETCH · follow-up screen study · 2026-07-19

The nano→full switch was worth it — for coverage, not readability.

A paired, judge-blind measurement of the follow-up-question screen FETCH shows after an opening query, with gpt-5-nano vs gpt-5.2 as the ensemble's OpenAI member and merge model.

373 scenarios 746 screens paired · blind judges: DeepSeek-V4 + Claude gemini + mistral held constant

gpt-5.2 reliably asks ~3 questions; gpt-5-nano asks nothing on roughly 1 in 4 screens. That coverage gap — not question readability or grounding — is what justifies the switch.

A claim that one tier writes better-grounded questions did not survive a second judge. When both arms do ask, their question quality is about the same.

01 · The decisive finding

nano stays silent a quarter of the time

For an intake tool whose whole job is to disambiguate a vague request, the worst outcome isn't an awkward question — it's no question at all. That is nano's dominant, judge-independent weakness.

Why is nano empty so often? (the surprising part)

It's a genuine generation effect, not a merge bug — the union of proposed questions is zero before any merge runs. Instrumenting each provider shows nano almost always returns a confident "no questions" verdict, and the other ensemble members don't reliably pick up the slack.

When nano is silent (usual) and gemini + mistral both happen to be silent too, the screen comes back empty. The follow-up screen is effectively the OpenAI member's output — which makes this a clean read on the swapped variable.

02 · The twist — and why two judges mattered

"Full writes worse questions" was a judge artifact

On the pairs where both arms asked something, the automated DeepSeek judge reported full as slightly worse — more presupposition, higher "unclear" rate. Read alone, that's a finding. It didn't replicate.

A blind Claude re-judge of a 30-scenario subset found the two arms statistically indistinguishable per question — and revealed that DeepSeek massively over-flags menu-style questions.

The two-judge design earned its keep.

A single automated judge would have shipped a wrong "full is worse on grounding" conclusion. The screens that FETCH does produce are mostly clean, single-topic, and heavily glossed — e.g. "medically stationary (not expected to get much better)".

03 · What is objectively different

full asks longer, denser screens

04 · Bottom line

Keep gpt-5.2 — for the right reason

Keep the switch. It's justified because nano abstains from asking anything ~24% of the time and the rest of the ensemble doesn't backfill — not because gpt-5.2 writes more readable or better-grounded questions.

Don't claim nano writes worse questions. When it does ask, its questions are about as clean as full's; the apparent grounding gap was a single-judge artifact.

The lever worth tuning next is full's density — it asks longer, more information-heavy screens. If reading load matters, that's the axis to prompt-tune, not grounding.
